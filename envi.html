ENVIRONMENT

CLIMATE CHANGE

  • Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns on Earth, driven largely by human activities since the Industrial Revolution, such as burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes. These activities emit greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, which trap heat in the atmosphere, leading to an increase in the planet's average surface temperature by about 1.5°C (2.5°F) since the 19th century.
  • This warming results in significant environmental impacts, including melting glaciers and sea ice, thawing permafrost, rising sea levels, and expanding deserts. The Arctic is warming the fastest, causing changes in ecosystems and forcing species to relocate or face extinction. Oceans absorb much of the excess heat, contributing to ocean warming and acidification, which threaten marine life and coral reefs.
  • Climate change also intensifies extreme weather events, resulting in more frequent and severe storms, heatwaves, droughts, and wildfires. These changes affect agriculture, water supplies, infrastructure, and human health, posing substantial risks globally.
  • Even if greenhouse gas emissions are drastically reduced, some effects like sea level rise and ocean acidification will continue for centuries because of the long-lasting nature of these gases and the delayed response of oceans.
  • Scientific consensus confirms that recent climate change is unprecedented in speed and magnitude relative to natural variations over millennia. Sophisticated satellite data and climate models have verified the link between human-induced greenhouse gas emissions and global warming.
  • Efforts to tackle climate change involve transitioning to renewable energy, protecting forests, and developing sustainable practices. However, the political influence of fossil fuel industries often poses challenges to effective climate action. The global community recognizes climate change as the greatest threat to sustainable development and emphasizes urgent, collective solutions to avoid irreversible damage to the planet.
  • In summary, climate change is a profound global challenge that threatens ecosystems, human societies, and the Earth's future livability, requiring immediate and sustained efforts worldwide to mitigate its impacts.
  • Global Temperature Rise: Earth's average surface temperature has increased about 1.5°C since the Industrial Revolution, with most warming occurring in the past 40 years. This trend is primarily driven by increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, now more than 50% higher than pre-industrial levels.
  • Record Heat: Years 2023 and 2024 have been the hottest on record globally, with 2024 reaching 1.28°C above 20th-century averages, causing extreme heatwaves and weather patterns.
  • Ecosystem Impacts: Climate change leads to severe effects such as desert expansion, melting of polar ice sheets, more intense storms, wildfires, droughts, and threats to biodiversity with many species forced to migrate or face extinction.
  • Sea Level Rise: Melting glaciers and thermal expansion of seawater are causing sea levels to rise, threatening coastal communities and increasing flooding risks worldwide. Long-term projections estimate several meters of sea level rise over millennia.
  • Tipping Points: Certain thresholds—such as the potential collapse of Greenland’s ice sheet or Atlantic Ocean circulation—could cause irreversible changes in climate systems with catastrophic impacts.

pollution

  • Air Pollution Release of harmful gases (like carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides), particulate matter (PM10, PM2.5), and chemicals into the atmosphere from sources such as vehicles, industries, agriculture, and natural events like wildfires.
  • Water Pollution Contamination of water bodies from industrial discharge, sewage, agricultural runoff (fertilizers, pesticides), and plastic waste, making water unsafe for humans and aquatic life.
  • Soil Pollution Occurs due to chemical spills, pesticide and herbicide application, heavy metals, and hydrocarbons contaminating soil and harming plant growth and soil organisms.
  • Noise Pollution Excessive noise from road traffic, aircraft, industrial activity, and high-intensity sonar negatively affecting human health and wildlife.
  • Plastic Pollution Accumulation of plastics and microplastics in environments like oceans and landfills, causing harm to wildlife and ecosystems.
  • Light Pollution Over-illumination and inappropriate outdoor lighting interfering with ecosystems, human health, and astronomical observations.
  • Thermal Pollution The alteration of natural water body temperatures, caused by activities such as discharging heated water from power plants, affecting aquatic life.
  • Visual Pollution Presence of unsightly man-made structures like billboards, overhead power lines, open dumps, and land scarring which degrade natural scenery.
  • Electromagnetic Pollution Excessive electromagnetic radiation from sources such as radio, Wi-Fi, and TV transmissions, which may affect electronic devices and some ecosystems.
  • Land Pollution Degradation of land surfaces due to improper disposal of waste, chemicals, and deforestation, leading to loss of fertile land and habitat.
  • Agricultural Pollution Chemicals from fertilizers, pesticides, and animal waste impacting air, water, and soil quality; methane emissions from livestock also contribute to air pollution.
  • Marine Pollution Pollution specifically affecting oceans and seas, including oil spills, plastic debris, toxic chemicals, and untreated sewage, threatening marine life and ecosystems.

Sustainability

  • Sustainability is the ability to maintain or support a process continuously over time, focusing on meeting present needs without compromising future generations.
  • It is often broken into three core pillars: economic, environmental, and social sustainability — also called profits, planet, and people.
  • Economic sustainability focuses on conserving natural resources to ensure long-term economic production.
  • Environmental sustainability emphasizes preserving life-support systems like soil, air, forests, and water to sustain human life and economic activities.
  • Social sustainability seeks to improve human well-being, eradicate poverty, combat hunger, and promote equality.
  • The 1987 Brundtland Report defined sustainable development as "meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs".
  • The United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) provide a global framework with 17 goals aimed at sustainable development, including no poverty, zero hunger, clean energy, climate action, and life on land and below water.
  • Goal 15 of the SDGs focuses on protecting terrestrial ecosystems, promoting afforestation, and halting biodiversity loss — key aspects of environmental sustainability.
  • Ensuring environmental sustainability requires strict enforcement of environmental laws, public participation, and social mobilization, as emphasized in policies like India’s Twenty Point Programme.
  • Social sustainability includes policies for the welfare of vulnerable groups such as elderly persons, disabled individuals, and orphans.
  • Sustainable management of forests, water, and biodiversity is critical for ecosystem health and human livelihoods, with ecosystem services valued at $125 trillion annually worldwide.
  • Water conservation through efficient use and xeriscaping is a vital part of sustainable living given increasing droughts and population pressures.
  • Reducing fast fashion and animal-based textiles supports sustainability by lowering carbon emissions and habitat destruction.

Conservation

  • Conservation preserves natural resources and biodiversity, maintaining the balance of ecosystems essential to life on Earth.
  • Protecting forests is critical because they are the planet’s lungs, home to half the terrestrial species, and help regulate climate and water cycles.
  • Planting trees and preventing deforestation help restore degraded land and combat climate change by absorbing carbon dioxide.
  • Water conservation is vital to sustain life, reduce scarcity, prevent pollution, and lower greenhouse gas emissions from water treatment and distribution.
  • Simple water-saving habits include turning off taps while brushing, taking shorter showers, fixing leaks, collecting rainwater, and running full loads in dishwashers and washing machines.
  • Wildlife conservation protects species from extinction, preserving genetic diversity and ecosystem services that humans rely on.
  • Preventive conservation anticipates potential environmental damage and acts early to avoid severe degradation in soils, water, and habitats.
  • Education and awareness are key to motivate collective action for conservation across communities and governments.
  • Sustainable use and management of resources ensure that cultural heritage, natural resources, and ecosystems remain intact for future generations.
  • Conservation actions include supporting protected areas, controlling forest fires, sustainable agriculture, and reducing pollution to ensure ecological equilibrium and human well-being.
